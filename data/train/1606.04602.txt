    "<S> we propose a simulation strategy which uses a classical device of linearly coupled chain of springs to simulate quantum dynamics , and obtain the quantum wave function from classical evolution . </S>",
    "<S> this goal is achieved with the classical momenta of the particles on the chain and their hilbert transform , from which we construct the many - body momentum and hilbert transformed momentum pair correlation functions yielding the real and imaginary parts of the wave function , respectively . with such wave function </S>",
    "<S> , we also find that the classical chain s energy and heat spreading densities can be related to the wave function s modulus square , thus providing a new perspective to understand ballistic heat transport . </S>",
    "<S> the results here give a definite answer to feynman s idea of using a classical device to simulate quantum physics .    in his pioneering work entitled `` simulating physics with computers ''  @xcite , r. feynman posed two important questions . </S>",
    "<S> first : what kind of computers are we going to use to simulate physics ? </S>",
    "<S> this led to the fundamental concept of a quantum computer  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the second was : can a quantum system be simulated by a classical computer ? </S>",
    "<S> the answer to this in the words of feynman is : `` no ! since this is called the hidden - variable problem : it is impossible to represent the results of quantum mechanics with a classical universal device '' . </S>",
    "<S> feynman also conditions this statement ( see precise details in  @xcite ) and writes that such rather far reaching conclusion is valid provided that there is no `` hocus - pocus '' . </S>",
    "<S> the aim of the present work is to theoretically build a classical device that can be used to simulate quantum dynamics . </S>",
    "<S> our device is a system of springs initially prepared at thermal equilibrium . </S>",
    "<S> the main difficulty recognized by feynman is , as he wrote : `` ... we can not simulate @xmath0 in the normal way '' , where @xmath0 is the wave function . indeed , </S>",
    "<S> quantum mechanics is built on interference and a complex valued field . </S>",
    "<S> of course , the interference is a wave property and for that reason we can imagine that vibrations are useful . but how can we get the complex valued wave function ? </S>",
    "<S> well , as we show below , the hocus - pocus is based on the hilbert transform  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the hilbert transform takes a real function @xmath1 and extends it to the complex plane in such a way that it satisfies the cauchy - riemann equations  @xcite , and it has been used extensively extending real signals to the complex plane . </S>",

    "<S> we have also found that this proposed wave function s modulus square corresponds to the classical energy and heat spreading densities . </S>",
