    "as main result of this paper , we have presented the effective fermion mass  ( [ fermion mass ] ) , dynamically generated in de sitter space . from the thermal features of de sitter space , and especially from the periodicity of the scalar green function in imaginary time  @xcite , one might expect that additional degrees of freedom coupled to the scalar field thermalise",
    ". this would be in analogy with an unruh detector , which has a thermal response function and , as a consequence of the principle of detailed balance , equilibrates at de sitter temperature .",
    "however , we find no indication of scatterings from a thermal bath or even thermalisation experienced by the fermion through interaction with the scalar field .",
    "the apparent reason is , that for an unruh detector , it is assumed that its internal dynamics is governed by its proper time and that detailed balance holds for the detector  @xcite .",
    "one might question whether this requirement may in principle be realised by an experimental device without disturbing the curved background to an extent which spoils the measurement .",
    "this is a concern which does not apply to the field theoretical investigation conducted in this work .",
    "nonetheless , the mass generation mechanism fits into the unruh detector picture when relating it to the lamb shift of the detector s energy levels  @xcite , which can also be interpreted as a radiative correction .",

    "<S> we study the one - loop radiative corrections for massless fermions in de sitter space induced by a yukawa coupling to a light , nearly minimally coupled scalar field . </S>",
    "<S> we show that the fermions acquire a mass . </S>",
    "<S> next we construct the corresponding ( nonlocal ) effective fermionic action , which  in contrast to the case of a massive dirac fermion  preserves chirality . </S>",
    "<S> nevertheless , the resulting fermion dynamics is precisely that of a dirac fermion with a mass proportional to the expansion rate . </S>",
    "<S> our finding supports the view that an observer or a test particle responds to a scalar field in inflation by shifting its energy rather than seeing a thermal bath . </S>"
