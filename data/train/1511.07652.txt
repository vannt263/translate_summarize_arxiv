    "the mc simulations are based on a novel model of biomembrane adhesion in which the membranes are represented as discretized elastic surfaces , and the receptors and ligands as anchored molecules that diffuse continuously along the membranes and rotate around their anchoring points .",

    "<S> adhesion processes of biological membranes that enclose cells and cellular organelles are essential for immune responses , tissue formation , and signaling . </S>",
    "<S> these processes depend sensitively on the binding constant @xmath0 of the membrane - anchored receptor and ligand proteins that mediate adhesion , which is difficult to measure in the ` two - dimensional ' ( 2d ) membrane environment of the proteins . </S>",
    "<S> an important problem therefore is to relate @xmath0 to the binding constant @xmath1 of soluble variants of the receptors and ligands that lack the membrane anchors and are free to diffuse in three dimensions ( 3d ) . in this article </S>",
    "<S> , we present a general theory for the binding constants @xmath0 and @xmath1 of rather stiff proteins whose main degrees of freedom are translation and rotation , along membranes and around anchor points ` in 2d ' , or unconstrained ` in 3d ' . </S>",
    "<S> the theory generalizes previous results by describing how @xmath0 depends both on the average separation and thermal nanoscale roughness of the apposing membranes , and on the length and anchoring flexibility of the receptors and ligands . </S>",
    "<S> our theoretical results for the ratio @xmath2 of the binding constants agree with detailed results from monte carlo simulations without any data fitting , which indicates that the theory captures the essential features of the ` dimensionality reduction ' due to membrane anchoring . in our monte carlo simulations , we consider a novel coarse - grained model of biomembrane adhesion in which the membranes are represented as discretized elastic surfaces , and the receptors and ligands as anchored molecules that diffuse continuously along the membranes and rotate at their anchor points . </S>"
