    "<S> a gambler walks into a hypothetical fair casino with a very real dollar bill , but by the time he leaves he s exchanged the dollar for a random amount of money . </S>",
    "<S> what is lost in the process ? </S>",
    "<S> it may be that the gambler walks out at the end of the day , after a roller - coaster ride of winning and losing , with his dollar still intact , or maybe even with two dollars . </S>",
    "<S> but what the gambler loses the moment he places his first bet is position . </S>",
    "<S> he exchanges one distribution of money for a distribution of lesser quality , from which he can not return . </S>",
    "<S> our first discussion in this work connects known results of economic inequality and majorization to the probability theory of gambling and martingales . </S>",
    "<S> we provide a simple proof that fair gambles can not increase the lorenz curve , and we also constructively demonstrate that any sequence of non - increasing lorenz curves corresponds to at least one martingale .    </S>",
    "<S> we next consider the efficiency of gambles . </S>",
    "<S> if all fair gambles are available then one can move down the lattice of distributions defined by the lorenz ordering . </S>",
    "<S> however , the step from one distribution to the next is not unique . </S>",
    "<S> is there a sense of efficiency with which one can move down the lorenz stream ? </S>",
    "<S> one approach would be to minimize the average total volume of money placed on the table . in this case </S>",
    "<S> , it turns out that implementing part of the strategy using private randomness can help reduce the need for the casino s randomness , resulting in less money on the table that the casino can not get its hands on .    </S>",
    "<S> fair gamble , gini index , lattice , lorenz curve , martingale convergence theorem . </S>"
