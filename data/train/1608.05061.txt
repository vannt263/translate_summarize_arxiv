    "<S> open physical systems with balanced loss and gain exhibit a transition , absent in their solitary counterparts , which engenders modes that exponentially decay or grow with time and thus spontaneously breaks the parity - time ( @xmath0 ) symmetry  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> this @xmath0-symmetry breaking is induced by modulating the strength  @xcite or the temporal profile  @xcite of the loss and gain , but also occurs in a pure dissipative system without gain  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> it has been observed that , in classical systems with mechanical  @xcite , electrical  @xcite , and electromagnetic setups  @xcite with static loss and gain , the @xmath0-symmetry breaking transition leads to extraordinary behavior  @xcite and functionalities  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> however , its observation in a quantum system is yet to be realized . </S>",
    "<S> here we report on the first quantum simulation of @xmath0-symmetry breaking transitions using ultracold @xmath1li atoms . </S>",
    "<S> we simulate static and floquet dissipative hamiltonians by generating state - dependent atom loss in a noninteracting fermi gas , and observe the @xmath0-symmetry breaking transitions by tracking the atom number for each state . </S>",
    "<S> we find that while the two - state system undergoes a single transition in the static case , its floquet counterpart , with a periodic loss , undergoes @xmath0-symmetry breaking and restoring transitions at vanishingly small dissipation strength . </S>",
    "<S> our results demonstrate that floquet dissipation offers a versatile tool for navigating phases where the @xmath0-symmetry is either broken or conserved . </S>",
    "<S> the dissipative ultracold fermi gas provides a starting point for exploring the interplay among dissipation , decoherence , and interactions in open quantum systems .    </S>",
    "<S> a fundamental postulate of the quantum theory is that the hamiltonian of a closed quantum system is hermitian . </S>",
    "<S> it ensures a real energy spectrum and guarantees unitary time evolution . </S>",
    "<S> two possibilities emerge when the quantum system is coupled to a dissipative environment . </S>",
    "<S> first , the coupling , such as that to a heat bath , leads to a loss of coherence in the system but preserves the trace of the density matrix , and it can be described by the lindblad formalism  @xcite . in the second scenario , </S>",
    "<S> the coupling leads to local losses in the system and is reflected in the diminishing trace of the density matrix . </S>",
    "<S> such a system is described by an effective , non - hermitian hamiltonian that encodes the energy ( or mass ) losses to the environment  @xcite .    in special cases , </S>",
    "<S> the lossy hamiltonian @xmath2 is equivalent to a hamiltonian @xmath3 that is invariant under combined parity ( @xmath4 ) and time - reversal ( @xmath5 ) operations  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> a key property shared by both hamiltonians is the @xmath0-symmetry breaking transition that occurs at an exceptional point  @xcite  a point in the parameter space where two resonant modes of the hamiltonian become degenerate  @xcite . by increasing the local loss in @xmath2 , this transition is predicted to reduce the overall dissipation rate . </S>",
    "<S> its counterpart , the @xmath0-symmetric hamiltonian @xmath3 models an open system with balanced loss and gain and allows the eigenmode spectrum to evolve from purely real to complex conjugate pairs at the transition . </S>",
    "<S> what is remarkable is that the @xmath0-symmetry breaking transition is expected to occur in an open , noninteracting , quantum two - state system  the simplest system possible . </S>",
    "<S> in contrast , in the closed - case , symmetry breaking transitions occur only in interacting quantum systems with many degrees of freedom .        </S>",
    "<S> ultracold atoms are powerful quantum simulators where impressive advances have been made towards simulating many - body physics by conserving coherence and minimizing losses  @xcite . </S>",
