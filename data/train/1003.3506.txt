    "<S> many proposals have been put forth for controlling quantum phenomena , including open - loop , adaptive feedback , and real - time feedback control . </S>",
    "<S> each of these approaches has been viewed as operationally , and even physically , distinct from the others . </S>",
    "<S> this work shows that all such scenarios inherently share the same fundamental control features residing in the topology of the landscape relating the target physical observable to the applied controls . this unified foundation may provide a basis for development of hybrid control schemes that would combine the advantages of the existing approaches to achieve the best overall performance </S>",
    "<S> .    </S>",
    "<S> steering the dynamics of quantum systems by means of external controls is a central goal in many areas of modern science , ranging from the creation of selective molecular transformations to quantum information processing @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the control may be either coherent ( e.g. , a shaped laser pulse  @xcite ) or incoherent ( e.g. , a tailored environment  @xcite or a sequence of quantum measurements  @xcite ) . </S>",
    "<S> quantum control is proving to be successful in the laboratory for manipulating a broad variety of physical , chemical , and biologically relevant processes  @xcite .    </S>",
    "<S> many seemingly distinct approaches have been developed for controlling quantum phenomena  @xcite , including open - loop control ( olc ) in which model - based designs are directly applied in experiments  @xcite , adaptive feedback control ( afc ) which is a measurement - guided closed - loop laboratory procedure including resetting the system to the initial state on each loop iteration  @xcite , and real - time feedback control ( rtfc ) which involves quantum measurement back - action on the system upon traversing the loop  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> each of these control schemes has been argued to be operationally , and even physically , distinct from the others . </S>",
    "<S> this rapid communication shows that all such scenarios are unified on a fundamental level by the common character of the control landscape  @xcite which relates the physical objective @xmath0 $ ] to the control variables @xmath1 . </S>",
    "<S> this general foundation makes it possible to extend the powerful landscape - based methods of optimality analysis , originally developed for olc and afc  @xcite , to rtfc . </S>",
    "<S> furthermore , one can envision combinations of such schemes @xcite , or even the prospect of new unanticipated ones , possibly arising in the future , all of which will share the same fundamental control landscape features .    </S>",
    "<S> the structure of the control landscape ( i.e. , its topology characterized by the nature of the local and global extrema ) determines the efficacy of a search for optimal solutions to the posed control problem  @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> searching for the global maximum of the objective function @xmath0 $ ] in the laboratory would be significantly hindered by the existence of local maxima that act as traps during the optimization procedure . </S>",
    "<S> even stochastic optimization algorithms could be impeded by a high density of traps @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> if the objective function has no traps , then , in principle , nothing lies in the way of reaching the global maximum ( i.e. , the best possible yield ) , except limited controls . </S>",
    "<S> this work establishes that a common landscape topology is shared by all quantum control schemes , leading to two general conclusions about controlling quantum phenomena . </S>",
    "<S> first , there are no local optima to act as traps on the landscape for a wide class of control problems , thereby enabling highly efficient searches for globally optimal controls . </S>",
    "<S> second , for each particular objective @xmath2 , there exists a single special control @xmath3 which is universally optimal for all initial states of a system ; this result implies that @xmath3 provides inherent robustness to variations of the initial system s state . for practical applications </S>",
    "<S> , these generic fundamental properties can facilitate more flexible laboratory implementations of quantum control as well as guide the design of suitable algorithms for laboratory optimization  @xcite .    </S>",
    "<S> the control landscapes for olc and afc were studied in recent works @xcite . for closed quantum systems , </S>",
    "<S> a control objective @xmath2 can be cast as a function of unitary evolution operators . </S>",
    "<S> assuming evolution - operator controllability ( i.e. , that any unitary evolution can be produced by the available controls ) , the landscapes were shown to have no traps for typical objectives , including the expectation value of an observable @xcite and the fidelity of a unitary quantum gate @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the absence of landscape traps was also analyzed for closed systems in the space of actual controls @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the landscapes for olc and afc of open quantum systems can be studied by casting an objective as a function of completely positive , trace - preserving maps ( i.e. , kraus maps @xcite ) which describe the system evolution . assuming kraus - map controllability ( i.e. , that any kraus map can be produced by the available controls ) </S>",

    "<S> are considered . the first one is based on feedback employing measurements of some quantum system output channel to guide a classical controller @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the other feedback approach is a quantum analog of watt s flyball governor  a self - regulating quantum machine . in this scenario ( called coherent rtfc ) , no measurements with a classical output signal are involved , and instead another quantum subsystem is employed to facilitate the control action , so that the evolution of the composite quantum system , consisting of the `` plant '' ( the subsystem of interest ) and `` controller '' ( the auxiliary subsystem ) , is coherent @xcite . </S>",
    "<S> the performance of practical rtfc controllers ( classical , quantum , or both ) can be , in principle , optimized using afc @xcite , possibly guided by an initial olc design , further enhancing the significance of a universal landscape topology common to all quantum control approaches .    in rtfc based on selective measurements of a single quantum system , </S>",
    "<S> the outcome of each measurement is random . after averaging over the random processes corresponding to all possible measurement outcomes , feedback control produces kraus - type evolution of the controlled system . in rtfc based on measurements of an ensemble of quantum systems , </S>",
    "<S> the ensemble average produces a kraus map as well . in coherent rtfc , if the plant and controller are initially prepared in a product state , the evolution of the plant is once again represented by a kraus map . </S>",
