    "<S> we present very large array observations at 1.4 and 5 ghz of a sample of 16 quasi - stellar objects ( qsos ) at @xmath0 to 2.71 . </S>",
    "<S> half of the chosen quasars are bright at mm wavelengths ( 250 or 350  ghz ) while the other half were not detected at mm wavelengths ; the former qsos were detected at 1.4  ghz , in most cases at high significance ( s / n  @xmath1  7 ) , but only three of the latter sources were detected at radio frequencies , and only at lower significance ( s / n  @xmath2  3 ) . </S>",
    "<S> the data are consistent with a correlation between the mm and radio fluxes indicating a physical connection between the mechanisms responsible for the radio and mm emission . </S>",
    "<S> however , this conclusion is based on data including many upper limits , and deeper data are clearly needed to verify this correlation . </S>",
    "<S> all eight mm detected qsos are detected in the radio continuum , with radio flux densities consistent with the radio - to - fir correlation for low z star forming galaxies . however , four of these have flatter spectral indices than is typical for star forming galaxies ( i.e. greater than -0.5 ) suggesting that radiation from the central agn dominates the observed radio emission . </S>",
    "<S> all the sources detected at 1.4 ghz are spatially unresolved , with the size limits typically @xmath3 kpc . </S>",
    "<S> high star formation rate galaxies at low redshift are typically nuclear starbursts , with sizes @xmath4 kpc . </S>",
    "<S> hence , the current radio size limits are insufficient to constrain the emission model ( agn or starburst ) . </S>"
